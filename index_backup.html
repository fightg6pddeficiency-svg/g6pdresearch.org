About Me

Luke Chen

I'm a student researcher with a deep personal connection to G6PD deficiency research. Living with G6PD deficiency myself, I've transformed a personal challenge into a mission to advance scientific understanding and help the 400+ million people worldwide who share this condition.

My G6PD Journey

I was diagnosed with G6PD deficiency as an infant when I experienced severe jaundice. Growing up, I learned to navigate a world full of hidden triggerscertain medications, foods like fava beans, and even some household chemicals could potentially cause dangerous hemolytic episodes. My mother and sister carry the G6PD gene, making this truly a family matter.

This personal experience drove me to create G6PD.ai, the world's first AI-powered safety checker for G6PD patients. But I knew that managing triggers wasn't enoughI wanted to understand the science deeply enough to contribute to finding a cure.

Research Vision

My approach combines computational biology with emerging gene editing technologies. By studying G6PD protein structures through AlphaFold and exploring CRISPR-Cas9 therapeutic strategies, I'm working to bridge the gap between structural biology and clinical intervention. This website documents my research journey and serves as a resource for others in the G6PD community.

Computational Biology

AlphaFold Protein Structure Analysis

Exploring G6PD protein structures using DeepMind's revolutionary AlphaFold AI to understand how mutations affect enzyme function and stability.

Understanding G6PD at the Molecular Level

G6PD (glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase) is a critical enzyme in the pentose phosphate pathway, essential for protecting red blood cells from oxidative stress. The enzyme functions as a dimer or tetramer, with each monomer containing three important binding sites.

Key Structural Features

  • Active Site: Where G6P substrate binds and catalysis occurs
  • Catalytic NADP+ Site: Cofactor binding for enzymatic reaction
  • Structural NADP+ Site: Critical for dimer stability (mutations here cause Class I deficiency)
  • Dimer Interface: Essential for enzyme activity; disruption causes severe deficiency

Why AlphaFold Matters

AlphaFold's ability to predict protein structures with atomic accuracy allows us to visualize how specific mutations destabilize the G6PD enzyme. This understanding is crucial for developing targeted therapies that could stabilize mutant enzymes or correct the underlying genetic defects.

What is Molecular Dynamics?

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations compute how atoms in a protein move over time by solving Newton's equations of motion. This allows us to observe protein behavior that's impossible to see experimentallylike how a mutation causes the protein to "breathe," unfold, or lose stability.

Drug Safety Science

Pharmacogenomics Analysis

Understanding at the molecular level why certain drugs trigger hemolysis in G6PD-deficient individualsand creating safer medication guidelines.

Why Do Certain Drugs Cause Hemolysis?

G6PD produces NADPH, which is essential for regenerating glutathionethe cell's primary defense against oxidative stress. Without sufficient G6PD activity, red blood cells cannot neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by certain drugs, leading to oxidative damage and hemolysis.

High Risk Drugs

Primaquine Antimalarial

Generates hydrogen peroxide directly in RBCs; contraindicated in all G6PD patients

Dapsone Antibiotic

Metabolized to hydroxylamine, a potent oxidant; dose-dependent hemolysis

Nitrofurantoin Urinary Antibiotic

Undergoes redox cycling, generating superoxide radicals

Rasburicase Uric Acid Reducer

Produces hydrogen peroxide as a byproduct; FDA black box warning

Moderate Risk Drugs

Sulfamethoxazole Antibiotic (Bactrim)

Risk varies by G6PD variant; may be safe in Class III at normal doses

Ciprofloxacin Fluoroquinolone

Occasional reports of hemolysis; generally considered low risk

Aspirin (high dose) NSAID

Low-dose aspirin appears safe; high doses may trigger hemolysis in severe variants

Vitamin K (water-soluble) Vitamin

Menadione form dangerous; phytonadione (K1) is safe

Safe Alternatives

Acetaminophen Pain Relief

Safe alternative to aspirin for pain and fever

Amoxicillin Antibiotic

No oxidative mechanism; safe in all G6PD variants

Azithromycin Antibiotic

Macrolide antibiotic; no hemolytic risk

Artemisinin Antimalarial

Safe alternative to primaquine for malaria treatment

Population Genetics

Evolutionary Biology: The Malaria Connection

Why does G6PD deficiency affect 400 million people? The answer lies in evolutionary pressure from malariaone of humanity's oldest diseases.

The Balanced Polymorphism Hypothesis

G6PD deficiency persists at high frequencies because it provides a survival advantage against Plasmodium falciparum malaria. This is similar to how sickle cell trait protects against malariaa classic example of heterozygote advantage or balancing selection.

Geographic overlap: G6PD deficiency prevalence mirrors historical malaria zones
58% reduced risk of severe malaria in G6PD-deficient individuals (Ruwende et al., Nature 1995)
Multiple G6PD variants arose independentlyconvergent evolution in response to malaria

How Does G6PD Deficiency Protect Against Malaria?

1
Reduced Parasite Growth

Malaria parasites require pentose phosphate pathway products for DNA synthesis. G6PD-deficient RBCs limit parasite replication.

2
Enhanced Phagocytosis

Ring-stage infected G6PD-deficient RBCs are more readily phagocytosed by macrophages, clearing infection earlier.

3
Oxidative Stress Sensitivity

Parasites are vulnerable to oxidative damage; the already-stressed environment in G6PD-deficient cells is hostile to them.

Research Proposal

Research Proposal

Experimental Validation of Computationally-Identified G6PD Stabilizers

Luke Chen | G6PD Research Initiative

Abstract

This proposal outlines a systematic approach to validate computationally-identified small molecule stabilizers of mutant G6PD enzymes. Using a combination of recombinant protein expression, thermal shift assays, and enzyme kinetics, we aim to identify pharmacological chaperones that could restore function to disease-causing G6PD variants.

Specific Aims

Aim 1
Express and purify recombinant G6PD variants

Clone wild-type and Mediterranean (S188F) G6PD in E. coli; purify using Ni-NTA affinity chromatography

Aim 2
Validate thermal stability enhancement

Use differential scanning fluorimetry (DSF) to measure Tm shift in presence of candidate compounds

Aim 3
Assess functional rescue

Measure enzyme kinetics (Km, Vmax) to determine if stabilizers restore catalytic function

Methods Overview

Protein Expression

BL21(DE3) E. coli, IPTG induction, His-tag purification

Thermal Shift Assay

SYPRO Orange dye, real-time PCR instrument, 25-95C gradient

Enzyme Kinetics

Spectrophotometric assay monitoring NADPH production at 340nm

Structural Validation

Circular dichroism to confirm proper folding

Timeline

Months 1-2 Cloning and protein expression optimization
Months 3-4 Purification and DSF assay development
Months 5-6 Compound screening (top 12 candidates)
Months 7-8 Kinetic characterization of hits
Months 9-10 Data analysis and manuscript preparation

Expected Outcomes

  • Identification of 2-3 compounds that increase G6PD Mediterranean thermal stability by 5C
  • Demonstration of partial activity restoration (target: 50% of wild-type Vmax)
  • Structure-activity relationship data to guide future compound optimization
  • Proof-of-concept for pharmacological chaperone approach in G6PD deficiency

Key Insights

Highest in Bone Marrow

G6PD is most highly expressed where red blood cells are produced. This explains why RBCs are most affectedthey need G6PD during their final maturation stage but cannot synthesize new enzyme once mature.

High in Metabolically Active Tissues

Liver, adrenal gland, and adipose tissue show high expression, reflecting G6PD's role in lipid synthesis (requires NADPH) and steroid hormone production.

X-Linked Expression

As an X-linked gene, females have two copies while males have one. Female heterozygotes show mosaic expression due to X-inactivation, explaining variable phenotypes in carrier women.

Systems Biology

Protein-Protein Interaction Network

Mapping G6PD's interactions with other proteins to understand its role in cellular networks and identify potential therapeutic targets.

Key Interaction Partners

GSR (Glutathione Reductase)
Uses NADPH from G6PD to regenerate reduced glutathione (GSH), the cell's primary antioxidant defense. Direct functional dependency.
PGD (6-Phosphogluconate Dehydrogenase)
Second enzyme in the oxidative PPP; receives 6-phosphogluconate from G6PD's product. Together they produce NADPH.
PRDX1 (Peroxiredoxin 1)
Antioxidant enzyme that detoxifies hydrogen peroxide. Requires NADPH (via thioredoxin reductase) for regeneration.
TKT & TALDO1 (Transketolase & Transaldolase)
Non-oxidative PPP enzymes that recycle pentose phosphates back to glycolysis. Work downstream of G6PD.
